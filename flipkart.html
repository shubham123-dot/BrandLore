The journey
The desi e-commerce revolution
2007
Two Engineers, One Flat, Zero Retail Experience
Sachin and Binny quit their Amazon jobs and start selling books online from a rented flat. They handle everything — coding, packaging, delivery, customer calls. Their first customer is a stranger in Mahbubnagar who orders a book. They courier it themselves. This hands-on approach teaches them what customers really want.
Do things that don't scaleCustomer obsession
2008
Cash on Delivery — The India Hack
India has deep trust issues with online payments. Most Indians don't have credit cards. Flipkart introduces Cash on Delivery (COD) — a radical trust-builder. This one feature unlocks 400 million potential customers overnight. It's not a feature, it's a market creation move. Indian e-commerce grows around this insight.
Market-specific innovationTrust architecture
2010
Building Logistics from Scratch
India's logistics infrastructure is broken. Courier companies are unreliable. Flipkart builds eKart — its own delivery network. This is expensive and painful. But it becomes their biggest moat. No competitor can replicate last-mile delivery to tier-2 and tier-3 cities. Infrastructure as competitive advantage.
Vertical integrationInfrastructure moat
2014
Big Billion Day — ₹600 Crore in 10 Hours
Flipkart launches India's first mega online sale. ₹600 crore in sales in one day. Servers crash. Stocks run out. Customers are angry. But the event proves that India will shop online — ferociously. It also teaches Flipkart how to handle scale. The second Big Billion Day is flawless. Iteration as strategy.
Event commerceFOMO marketing
2016
Myntra + Jabong — Acquire to Win
Rather than build a fashion vertical from scratch, Flipkart acquires Myntra for $330M, then Jabong for $70M. This collapses the competitive landscape instantly. Amazon hasn't entered fashion seriously yet. Flipkart uses capital strategically to buy time and market share. In winner-take-all markets, acquisition beats organic growth.
Acquisition strategyCategory expansion
2018
Walmart Pays $16 Billion — India's Biggest Exit
Walmart acquires 77% of Flipkart for $16B — the largest e-commerce acquisition in history at the time. Two engineers from IIT Delhi who started with ₹4 lakh hand over a $21B company. Sachin and Binny had essentially built the rails of Indian e-commerce. Walmart was buying the infrastructure, not just the company.

The playbook
6 Strategies That Built Flipkart
01
Solve for India, Not the West
Cash on Delivery, regional language support, EMI on everything, easy returns — Flipkart didn't copy Amazon's Western playbook. They rewrote it for Indian realities. Low internet penetration, trust issues, no credit card culture, bad roads — every constraint became a product decision. Localisation is not translation; it's redesign.
→ Unlocked 400M+ new customers
02
Build the Invisible Infrastructure
eKart (logistics), PhonePe (payments), Myntra (fashion) — Flipkart built the invisible plumbing of Indian commerce. This wasn't the exciting, visible part of the business. But it was the part that was impossible to replicate. When Amazon entered India, Flipkart's logistics network was years ahead. The boring stuff is the moat.
→ Logistics impossible to replicate quickly
03
Grow the Category, Not Just the Company
Flipkart's early marketing didn't just promote Flipkart — it promoted online shopping itself. "Ab India karega online shopping." When you're in an emerging market, your first job is to make people believe the category exists. Category creation is more valuable than market share — because the whole pie is bigger.
→ Created India's ₹5 lakh crore e-commerce market
04
Use Capital as Competitive Weapon
Flipkart raised $7B+ before profitable. They used capital to discount aggressively, acquire competitors, and build infrastructure. When Amazon entered India with $2B, Flipkart was already entrenched in seller relationships, logistics, and customer trust. Capital buys time — and time buys the learning advantage.
→ Defended market share for 5+ years
05
Event Commerce — Create Urgency
The Big Billion Day created urgency and habit simultaneously. Indians started planning purchases around Flipkart sales events. This is a behaviour change — infinitely more valuable than a single sale. When customers change their calendar for you, you've become infrastructure. Scarcity + timing = habit formation.
→ Billions in GMV per event
06
Hire the Ecosystem
Flipkart hired from Amazon, McKinsey, and IIMs — people who'd seen what scale looked like. But they also promoted from within obsessively. Kalyan Krishnamurthy (now CEO) came from Tiger Global. The Flipkart mafia — alumni who've founded 100+ startups — is arguably their greatest output. Your team is your product.
→ Built India's deepest startup talent pool
We want to transform commerce in India. We want to make it possible for a small business in a village in Bihar to sell to the whole world.

For founders
What Flipkart teaches every Indian startup
🇮🇳
India is not a smaller America
The biggest mistake Indian startups make is copying Western models without adapting them. Flipkart won because it solved specifically Indian problems. Study your market deeply before importing solutions from elsewhere.
🔧
Do the unglamorous work
Building logistics is slow, expensive, and boring. But it's what made Flipkart unbeatable. The work no one wants to do is usually the work that creates the strongest moats. Don't just build the product — build the infrastructure around it.
⚡
Speed is a strategy
Flipkart moved fast — sometimes too fast (Big Billion Day crash). But speed created habits before competitors arrived. In emerging markets with no incumbents, the first credible player wins. Being fast and fixing mistakes is better than being perfect and being late.
🤝
Build trust before you build revenue
COD was expensive for Flipkart — high return rates, cash handling costs. But it built trust in a country that had none for online shopping. Trust is the product in markets where the category is new. Revenue follows trust, not the other way around.
