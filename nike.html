The journey
The unlikely empire of sport
1964
Selling Shoes from a Car Boot
Phil Knight, fresh from Stanford MBA, imports Onitsuka Tiger shoes from Japan and sells them at track meets from the boot of his car. He spots a gap: American runners have no good, affordable shoes. His first year revenue: $8,000. His first distribution channel: athletic events where his customers already were.
Go where your customers areImport arbitrage
1971
Nike Is Born — $35 Swoosh Included
After falling out with Onitsuka, Knight renames the company Nike (Greek goddess of victory) and commissions graphic design student Carolyn Davidson to create a logo for $35. Knight says "I don't love it, but it'll grow on me." It grows on 8 billion people. The lesson: great brands are built over time, not in a meeting.
Identity creationSimple, scalable logo
1984
The Michael Jordan Bet — $250K That Changed Everything
Jordan wanted Adidas. Nike offers a $250K/year deal and a signature shoe — unheard of in 1984. The Air Jordan 1 launches. The NBA bans it for violating uniform rules. Nike pays the $5,000-per-game fine. The controversy is priceless publicity. Air Jordan becomes a $4 billion brand within 35 years. One athlete. One bet.
Athlete-as-brandControversy as marketing
1988
"Just Do It" — Three Words, Infinite Power
Ad agency Wieden+Kennedy creates "Just Do It" — inspired (ironically) by a murderer's last words. The slogan transcends sport entirely. It's not about shoes — it's about overcoming your own limitations. Nike's sales go from $877M to $9.2B in the next decade. Three words carry more weight than most brand strategies combined.
Universal messagingEmotional positioning
1997
Tiger Woods & the "Hello World" Ad
Nike signs the 21-year-old Tiger Woods for $40M. Days into his pro career, Nike airs "Hello World" — a stark ad about racism in golf. "There are still courses in the U.S. I am not allowed to play because of the colour of my skin." Brave, polarising, unforgettable. Nike repeatedly chooses sides rather than playing it safe.
Value-led marketingBrave storytelling
2018
Colin Kaepernick — "Believe in Something"
Nike makes Kaepernick the face of their 30th anniversary "Just Do It" campaign — the quarterback who knelt during the anthem. #NikeBoycott trends. Shares drop 3%. Then reverse. Nike's online sales jump 31% in 3 days. Stock hits an all-time high. Nike had modelled the risk. They knew their core customer would celebrate, not leave.

The playbook
6 Strategies That Built Nike
01
Athlete as Brand Ambassador
Nike didn't just sponsor athletes — they co-created identities. Air Jordan wasn't a shoe endorsement; it was a sub-brand. LeBron, Serena, Ronaldo — each has a Nike line that functions as a brand within a brand. Fans of the athlete become fans of Nike without Nike ever asking. Borrowed credibility compounds over time.
→ Air Jordan alone = $4B annual revenue
02
Sell Aspiration, Not Equipment
Nike's target customer is not elite athletes — it's people who want to feel like one. "Just Do It" speaks to the 5am runner, the gym beginner, the weekend warrior. Every Nike ad shows someone overcoming something. The product is a physical object. The brand is a permission slip to believe in yourself.
→ 40% of customers never play sport seriously
03
The Asset-Light Manufacturing Model
Nike owns no factories. All manufacturing is outsourced to Asia. This was radical in the 1970s and is now the standard for every major fashion brand. Nike focuses capital on design, marketing, and athlete relationships — the high-value parts. Manufacturing is a commodity. Creativity is not. Focus resources accordingly.
→ Industry-leading 45% gross margins
04
Innovation as Marketing
Air cushioning. Flyknit. React foam. Vaporfly carbon plate. Every Nike technology launch is a PR event. When Vaporfly shoes helped marathon runners break records, the story ran in every newspaper. Nike didn't buy the ad — the innovation earned it. Product innovation that generates press is the most efficient marketing spend possible.
→ Vaporfly controversy = $0 in ads, global coverage
05
Scarcity and Hype Drops
Nike mastered the art of releasing limited shoes in small quantities — creating queues, resale markets, and cultural events. A shoe that sells for $180 retail trades for $2,000 on StockX. Nike gets the cultural cachet without giving up margin. Scarcity makes demand permanent. When everyone wants something fewer people can have, desire compounds.
→ Nike resale market worth $6B globally
06
Take Calculated Controversial Stands
Kaepernick. Serena. Colin. Nike repeatedly takes public positions on cultural issues — and it consistently pays off. This is not recklessness. Nike studies its customer base deeply. They know that Gen Z and millennials expect brands to have values. Silence is a position. Nike chose to be explicit. Clarity of values builds fanatical loyalty.
→ 31% sales jump after Kaepernick campaign
The cowards never started and the weak died along the way. That leaves us, ladies and gentlemen. Us.
— Phil Knight, Shoe Dog (Nike memoir)
For founders
What Nike teaches every startup
🎯
Find your Michael Jordan early
Every startup has an equivalent of a celebrity endorser — a power user, an influencer, an expert, a community. Find the one person whose credibility, if attached to your product, changes everything. Pursue them obsessively. Offer them equity, not just money.
💬
Three words beat three paragraphs
Nike's entire brand fits in three words. Can you say what your startup does in three words? If not, your positioning isn't sharp enough. Simplicity scales. Complexity confuses. Work on your tagline as hard as you work on your product.
🏭
Own the magic, outsource the rest
Nike owns design, marketing, and athlete relationships. Everything else is outsourced. What is the "magic" in your business — the part that creates all the value? Own that fiercely. Outsource everything else to stay lean and focused.
🔥
Have the courage to have values
Brands that stand for nothing are forgotten. The Kaepernick campaign was a calculated risk — but it was also genuine. Nike's customers knew it. Authentic values attract loyal communities. Performative values repel everyone. Know what you believe before you communicate it.
